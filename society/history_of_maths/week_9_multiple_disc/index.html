Multiple discoveries and misattributions

Peter Rowlett

Stigler’s Law

  • Stigler’s Law says that no scientific discovery is named after its original discoverer.

  • Stephen Stigler names Robert K. Merton as the discoverer of ‘Stigler’s Law’, meaning it is an example of Stigler’s Law.

  • (To be clear: this is not entirely serious!)

Brown’s Law

  • Stigler’s Law is open to disproof by counterexample.

  • Brown proposed this more mathematical formulation in 2007, first discovered by Carl Boyer in 1968 (Brown and Rice, 2022):

    Theorem (Brown’s Law)

    Let \(n\) be the total number of named theorems and formulae in mathematics and \(p(n)\) be the number of these results that are named after the person (or people) who originally discovered them. Then

    \[ \lim _{n \to \infty } \frac{p(n)}{n} = 0 \text{.} \]

Example

  • \((\cos (x) + \mathrm{i} \sin (x))^n = \cos (nx) + \mathrm{i} \sin (nx)\) is known as de Moivre’s theorem. But the first proof was given by Euler.

  • Euler was not the first to discover Euler’s formula \(\mathrm{e}^{\mathrm{i}x} = \cos (x) + \mathrm{i} \sin (x)\). An equivalent form was proved by Roger Cotes.

  • Cotes was one of several people to make use of Taylor series, named after Brook Taylor who was not the first to discover these.

  • The related concept of Maclaurin series were not first published by Colin Maclaurin (who published Cramer’s rule two years before Cramer). One of those who used Maclaurin series earlier was James Stirling.

  • Stirling’s approximation, \(n! \approx \sqrt(2\pi n) \left(\frac{n}{e}\right)^n \) for large \(n\), was in fact discovered by Abraham de Moivre!(Brown and Rice, 2022)

What’s going on?

Generative Al models “are designed to generate text based on patterns and examples present in the data they were trained on. They do not have an inherent understanding of truth or ethics and are agnostic to the factual accuracy of the information they generate.”

— New Scientist (16 Sept 2023, p. 38) – quoting a response from ChatGPT.

  • Fundamentally, these AI programs are “stochastic parrots”: “haphazardly stitching together sequences of linguistic forms it has observed in its vast training data, according to probabilistic information about how they combine, but without any reference to meaning” (Bender, et al., 2021).

Appropriate use

  • The only reason I can spot these errors is because I know the topics.

  • How did I develop this ability? Not by writing queries to AI.

  • These tools are very good at text processing.

  • They are not good at facts.

  • Use the right tool for the right job.

Multiple discoveries

Multiple discovery

  • Sometimes, the same mathematical idea is discovered more than once.

  • Sometimes, this is nearly at the same time, causing a priority dispute.

  • Other times, this is an idea which was previously known in another place a long time ago.

Why does this happen?

  • Perhaps because people don’t name ideas after themselves, others do.

  • This makes it likely that credit will attach to a well-known person who brings a concept to greater attention, rather than the original person.

Example: Stokes’ Theorem

  • Named after George Gabriel Stokes because of his tendency of including it on Cambridge prize examinations.

  • However, the first statement of the theorem was by William Thompson (Lord Kelvin) in a letter to Stokes.

  • Clearly, Stokes would have been aware of Kelvin’s work and presumably didn’t claim to have originated the theorem.

Newton/Leibniz

  • A famous priority dispute occurred between Isaac Newton and Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz over the invention of the calculus.

  • They probably had discovered calculus independently.

  • This led to an investigation by the Royal Society and a dispute that effectively separated English mathematicians from those in continental Europe for many years.

Discovery of previously-known ideas

  • There are many concepts that were discovered in Europe, where we get our name for them, despite having been known previously by non-European cultures.

  • For example, Pascal’s triangle, named for Blaise Pascal (1623–1662), though it had been studied centuries earlier in India, Persia, China and even in other parts of Europe.

  • The Fibonacci sequence had been studied in India centuries before Fibonacci.

  • It may be that Pascal and Fibonacci rediscovered something forgotten, or that they knew about the idea from the earlier source.

Not always advancing

  • We tend to think of mathematics as a body of knowledge to which new ideas are added.

  • Ideas that are discovered, forgotten and then subsequently rediscovered, perhaps centuries later, give us the notion that maths isn’t an ever-advancing, coherent body of knowledge.

  • Instead, it may be that parts of maths are forgotten and lost; that we are not always advancing.

  • Treating older mathematics as more primitive and assuming that we are always adding to our body of knowledge over time is a trap to be aware of when reading and writing about history.
