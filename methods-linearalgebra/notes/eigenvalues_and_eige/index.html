1.1 Background

Example 1

Representing this as an augmented matrix

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 5& -3& 0\\ 10& -2& 0 \end{array}\right] \]

we can replace row 2 with \(2 \times \)row 1 minus row 2 to obtain

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 5& -3& 0\\ 0& -8& 0 \end{array}\right] \]

Row 2 gives \(-8y = 0 \implies y=0\), so the only solution possible is the trivial one, \((x,y)=(0,0)\).

Example 2

Again, as an augmented matrix:

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 5& -3& 0\\ 10& -6& 0 \end{array}\right] \]

Replacing row 2 with \(2 \times \)row 1 minus row 2 gives

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 5& -3& 0\\ 0& 0& 0 \end{array}\right]\text{.} \]

This is consistent and tells us that the second equation is a linear combination of the first (in fact, it is double the first). If we let \(y=t\) then by row 1 we have \(x = \frac{3}{5}t\). There is an infinite family of solutions of the form \((x,y) = (\frac{3}{5}t,t)\) for \(t \in \mathbb {R}\).

1.3 Definition

Let \(\mathbf{A}\) be an \(n\times n\) matrix. Then the number \(\lambda \) is an eigenvalue of \(\mathbf{A}\) if there exists a non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v}\) such that

\[ \mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}=\lambda \mathbf{v}\text{.} \]

Then \(\mathbf{v}\) is an eigenvector of \(\mathbf{A}\) corresponding to \(\lambda \).
 
Rewrite \(\mathbf{A}\mathbf{v}=\lambda \mathbf{v}\) as

Now, we require \(\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I}\) must not be invertible, otherwise

and we wanted non-trivial solutions.

Recall that a square matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) is invertible if and only if \(\det (\mathbf{A}) \ne 0\).
 
So we require

\[ \det \left(\mathbf{A} - \lambda \mathbf{I}\right)=0\text{.} \]

We call \(p(\lambda )=\det (\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I})\) the characteristic polynomial of \(\mathbf{A}\).

2 Diagonalisation

2.1 Definition

Sometimes it can be easier to handle problems involving square matrices by transforming them to easier problems about diagonal matrices.

A diagonal matrix is one where the only non-zero entries are on the main diagonal (top left to bottom right).

To diagonalise a matrix \(\mathbf{A}\) means to find an invertible matrix \(\mathbf{U}\) and a diagonal matrix \(\mathbf{D}\) such that

\[ \mathbf{A} = \mathbf{U}\mathbf{D}\mathbf{U}^{-1}\text{.} \]

If we have a set of eigenvalues and eigenvectors for \(\mathbf{A}\), we can achieve this by forming \(\mathbf{U}\) using the eigenvectors as columns and forming \(\mathbf{D}\) by including the corresponding eigenvalues as the entries along the main diagonal.

2.2 Example

Earlier we saw that

\[ \mathbf{A}=\left[\begin{array}{cc} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 \\ \end{array}\right] \]

had eigenvalues \(\lambda _1=6\) and \(\lambda _2=1\) with corresponding eigenvectors \(\mathbf{v}_1=\left[\begin{array}{c} 1 \\ 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\) and \(\mathbf{v}_2=\left[\begin{array}{c} -4 \\ 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\).

So we can diagonalise \(\mathbf{A}\) using

We must compute \(\mathbf{U}^{-1}\). This is

\[ \mathbf{U}^{-1} = \frac{1}{1\times 1 - -4 \times 1} \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 4 \\ -1 & 1 \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cc} \frac{1}{5} & \frac{4}{5} \\ -\frac{1}{5} & \frac{1}{5} \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \]

We can check

\[ \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1 & -4 \\ 1 & 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc} 6 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc} \frac{1}{5} & \frac{4}{5} \\ -\frac{1}{5} & \frac{1}{5} \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cc} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 5 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \]

One example is if you want to compute \(\mathbf{A}^n\) for arbitrary \(n\).

Now consider \(\mathbf{D}^n\).

If \(\mathbf{D}= \left[\begin{array}{cc} a & 0 \\ 0 & b \\ \end{array}\right]\) then

\[ \mathbf{D}^2= \left[\begin{array}{cc} a & 0 \\ 0 & b \\ \end{array}\right]\left[\begin{array}{cc} a & 0 \\ 0 & b \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{cc} a^2 & 0 \\ 0 & b^2 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \]

\[ \mathbf{D}=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} a_1 & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & a_2 & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & a_k \end{array}\right]\text{,} \]

\[ \mathbf{D}^n=\left[\begin{array}{cccc} a_1^n & 0 & \ldots & 0 \\ 0 & a_2^n & \ldots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \ldots & a_k^n \end{array}\right]\text{.} \]
