Proof methods – exercises

Peter Rowlett

  1. Prove or disprove the following.

    1. Let \(m\) be an integer. If \(m\) is odd, then \(m^2\) is odd.

    2. Suppose that \(p \in \mathbb {Q}\) and \(p^2 \in \mathbb {Z}\). Then, \(p \in \mathbb {Z}\).

    3. Let \(m\) and \(n\) be real numbers. If \(n {\gt} m {\gt} 0\), then

      \[ \frac{m+1}{n+1} {\gt} \frac{m}{n}\text{.} \]

    4. Let \(A\), \(B\) and \(C\) be sets. Then,

      \[ A \cap (B \cup C) = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap C)\text{.} \]

    5. Let \(A\) and \(B\) be sets. Then, \(A=B\) if and only if \(A \subseteq B\) and \(B \subseteq A\).

  2. Show that the sum of two consecutive odd numbers is a multiple of 4. What is the converse and is it true?

  3. Let \(f: X \to Y\). Suppose \(A\) and \(B\) are subsets of \(X\). Show \(f(A \cup B) = f(A) \cup f(B)\).

  4. Prove or disprove the following.

    1. \(a + b = c \implies a^2 + b^2 = c^2\).

    2. Let \(x \in \mathbb {Z}\). Then \(-x\) is negative.

  5. The following is a proof that \(1=2\). You may have reason to doubt that this is a true fact! Where is the error?

    Theorem

    \(1=2\).

    Proof.

    Let \(a=b\), where \(a,b \in \mathbb {Z}\). Then,

    \[ \begin{array}{rcll} ab & =& a^2 & \text{since $a=b$,}\\ ab-b^2 & =& a^2-b^2 & \text{by subtracting $b^2$ from both sides,}\\ b(a-b) & =& (a+b)(a-b) & \text{by factoring,}\\ b & =& a+b & \text{by dividing both sides by $a-b$,}\\ b & =& 2b & \text{since $a=b$,}\\ 1 & =& 2 & \text{by dividing by $b$.} \end{array} \]

    □


  6. Prove or disprove the following.

    1. Suppose \(n \in \mathbb {N}\). Then \(n^3-n\) is a multiple of \(3\).

    2. Suppose \(x,y \in \mathbb {R}\). Then \(|x+y| \le |x| + |y|\).

    3. The square of any integer is of the form \(3k\) or \(3k+1\) for some \(k \in \mathbb {Z}\).

    4. Suppose \(a=bc\) for \(a,b,c \in \mathbb {R}\). If two of \(a\), \(b\) or \(c\) are non-zero, then so is the third.

  7. Prove or disprove the following.

    1. There are no positive integers \(x\) and \(y\) such that \(x^2-y^2 =1\).

    2. The sum of a rational and an irrational number is an irrational number.

    3. \(\sqrt{3}\) is irrational.

    4. \(\sqrt{4}\) is irrational. (What happens is you try the same approach as for \(\sqrt{3}\)?)

    5. There are no positive integer solutions to \(x^2+x+1=y^2\).

    6. Suppose \(x,y \in \mathbb {Z}\). Then \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2} \ne x+y\).
